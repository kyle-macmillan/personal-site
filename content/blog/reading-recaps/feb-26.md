## Books

What can I say about **Moby Dick**? It bested me twice before,
and for years sat taunting me on the shelf. At each attempt, the 
opening scenes, especially Queequeg and Ishmael's first night
together, would enthrall me. But as soon as I hit *Cetalogy*, things
ground to a halt. It was an unwelcome diversion from the main event. 

This time, rather than dread and work my way through these chapters, 
I took them in as necessary parts of the story. And good thing too, 
because there were many more. 

What's funny in hindsight is that these chapters never did feel out  of place.
Not just because they contextualized the business of  whaling---that level of
detail seems unnecessary to understand the main story---but also because they
balance  the image of whales. It's all too easy for THE whale (i.e. 
Moby Dick) to get caught up in symbolism and allegory so the factual 
chapters bring him back to earth. 

---

It's a bit embarassing to call Moby Dick more of an experience than a 
book but that's how it felt to read. It's easy to get sucked in and 
feel in awe of the story and prose. I'll read a sentence that's better
than anything I've ever written and realize I'm only on page 3. There's
a poetic, theatrical quality. An example:

After Captain Ahab reveals the Pequod's true mission to his crew, Starbuck,
his first mate, has reservation.

*"God keep me!--keep us all!" murmured Starbuck, lowly.*

*But in his joy at the enchanted, tacit acquiesence of the mate, Ahab 
did not hear his foreboding invocation; nor yet the low laugh from the
hold; nor yet the presaging vibrations of the winds in the cordage; nor
yet the hollow flap of the sails against the masts, as for a moment their
hearts sank in. For again Starbuck's downcast eyes lighted up with the
stubborness of life; the subterranean laugh died away; the winds blew on;
the sails filled out; the ship heaved and rolled as before. Ah, ye 
admonitions and warnings! why stay ye not when ye come? But rather are ye
predictions than warnings, ye shadows! Yet not so much predictions from
without, as verifications of the foregoing things within. For with little
external to constrain us, the innermost necessities in our being, these 
still drive us on.*
