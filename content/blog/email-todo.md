---
title: "Using Email as TODO"
date: '2026-06-29'
tags: ["misc"]
---

Over the years I've tried many different ways of keeping a todo list. I started with a nice
Traveler's notebook, but never got in the habit of using it. In hindsight, having a reminder to use
the notebook would have worked, but the idea of needing a meta-productivity device is somewhat
off-putting. 

Seeing as I work on my computer (basically) all day, I thought I'd have better luck building a habit
with an electronic to do list. There's tons of productivity software, but, like the notebook, I
never got to the point where opening the todo list became part of my routine. The closest I got was
having a single markdown file `TODO.md` that I could open in the terminal with the alias `todo`. 
Charitably, I got halfway there. I got in the habit of adding to the todo list, but not checking the
to-do list. 

Once I realized my problem was twofold, using email came naturally. Any time I have a task, I email
myself with `[TODO]` in the subject line, and the message is routed to a special TODO inbox that
sits at the top of the page. I've been using this approach for about a month and it is by far and
away the best that I've tried. 

If there's any design or organizational principle to take away, it's to minimize tool bloat. In my
(academic) experience, Slack is unnecessary when email exists. Pick one of Google Docs and Overleaf.
Better yet, just use Github for everything. Resolving a merge conflict isn't the end of the world,
and may even provoke good discussion.
