# A Primer on Provenance in Databases
{{< side-image
  src="/img/blog/diana-actaeon.jpg"
  caption="Diana and Actaeon by Titian (1556-1559). According to Wikipedia, the work's full provenance is known."
>}}

Although research on data provenance extends back decades, increasing
commodification and sharing of data has placed new salience on provenance.
Knowing, inter alia, the origin, chain of ownership, and what transformations
the data has undergone is useful across fields. For example, 
having a complete provenance can 
- prevent (or catch) the misappropriation of proprietary or private data,
- help data users better understand their data,
- audit data sharing practices between organizations. 

Given provenance's growing applicability, this blog post provides a short
introduction to provenance in relational databases. To be sure, capturing
the provenance of data stored in other formats is important.{{< sidenote >}}
See, e.g., [The C2PA's standard for provenance for images](https://c2pa.org/).
{{< /sidenote >}} But relational databases continue to be one of the most
commonly used models and, by all indications, are here to stay.

For a more complete treatment of the topics covered in this post, check out
[Provenance in Databases: Why, How, and Where](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1561/1900000006) 
by Cheney, Chiticariu, and Tan, as well as 
[Data Provenance](https://www.nowpublishers.com/article/Details/DBS-068) by
Glavic.
