Introduction

Although research on data provenance extends back decades, increasing commodification and sharing of data has placed new salience on provenance. Knowing, inter alia, the origin, chain of ownership, and what transformations the data has undergone is useful across fields. For example, having a complete provenance can

  • prevent (or catch) the misappropriation of proprietary or private data,
  • help data users better understand their data,
  • audit data sharing practices between organizations.

Given provenance’s growing applicability, this blog post provides a short introduction to provenance in relational databases. To be sure, capturing the provenance of data stored in other formats is important. See, e.g., The C2PA’s standard for provenance for images. But relational databases continue to be one of the most commonly used models and, by all indications, are here to stay.

For a more complete treatment of the topics covered in this post, check out Provenance in Databases: Why, How, and Where by Cheney, Chiticariu, and Tan, as well as Data Provenance by Glavic.
