Will the Real Gen Z Please Stand Up

In the age of big data, generation groups seem almost quaint. Despite the daily classification of individuals into ever finer classes by tech companies, Male, 27, afraid of heights. we continue to generalize about the tendencies of people born more than a decade apart. As somone on the borderline of two groups, associating with either feels absurd. Too young to remember 9/11, too old for ChatGPT in school, just in time for Diary of a Wimpy Kid (the book). How much do I really have in common with a 13-year old?

If we insist on keeping age-based descriptors, it seems more natural to mark cutoffs based on major societal events. After all, that’s how we used to do things. Each of the earliest generational groups were defined in relationship to one of the world wars. The Lost Generation for WWI, the Greatest Generation for WWII. But without any more world wars to mark the passage of time, the folks in charge of defining generations groups ran into trouble. Starting with Gen X, Formerly the “Baby Bust” generation. each generation seems to last 15 years, regardless of what goes on in the world.

There is some sense to this heuristic. Fifteen years is roughly the time it takes to go from first grade to college graduation. But again, there’s little commonality betweeen a new grad entering the work force and a child learning multiplication, especially when it comes to opinion polling, which is the main use case for generation groups.

It’s time we return to our roots and define generations according to events. Although we haven’t had any more world wars, we’re not lacking in generation-defining events: 9/11, the 2008 recession, COVID, maybe even AI. And the names must get better. Generation Alpha? Wouldn’t want to be in Generation Beta.
